The primary goal of eradicating consumptive screen time is using that time towards something productive instead (like work, health, or relationships). There are definitely other qualitative mental health benefits than the time saved (speaking from personal experience) but the scope of this post is the time saved only. So we will primarily be tackling consumptive screen time categories that have the most potential to steal away long chunks of your time.

I believe hard environmental changes and digital mechanisms are the 2 * (make that split clear in the solution sections below? also are those terms apt?) best tools towards achieving that goal as opposed to will power. But I do not believe in buying the so called dumb phones. Just get 1 regular laptop and 1 regular smart phone as they are essential to modern life.

For me, consumptive screen time usually falls under these 3 categories: doom scrolling, TV, YouTube. It is essential that you become aware of what your categories are. For example, yours could be video games. The rest of this post is specific to my categories, please adapt it to your specific predilections.

Doom scrolling in this context means any screen where you can keep on scrolling infinitely. For me, this includes apps like Instagram/Reels, YouTube/Shorts, X/Twitter, Reddit, TeamBlind, LinkedIn, etc. I also used to waste a lot of time on HackerNews even though it is technically not doom scrolling. But I mostly only wasted time on the comments section so I try to block that specifically.

Do not keep any native applications on your phone/laptop that allow for consumptive screen time except for one web browser (as it is essential for modern life). Figure out a way to block individual websites through browser extensions (like LeechBlock) or through other provisions that are sometimes there on an operating-system level.

For me, it was easiest to de-addict myself from the nastiest culprits. Like Reels/Shorts/Twitter. Some other things like HackerNews/YouTube fall under a grey area where you DO derive some value from parts of the experience. These need to be blocked selectively.

Do not deal in absolutes. For example, if you doom scroll Reddit, realize that instead of blocking all of Reddit, you can block only the doom scrolling page type while still having the post page type allowed. (For times when you Google something that leads to a Reddit post).

YouTube has a web browser extension called Unhook that allows you to remove all shorts and recommendations and redirects you to your subscribed videos only. If you subscribe judiciously to high quality YouTube channels you will realize quickly enough that it is hard to waste time on this system because interesting uploads are quite sparse even if you have dozens of subscriptions. If an extension does not exist for removing addictive elements from your specific website, ask AI to write a tampermonkey script for it.

Speaking of which, do not use AI for long drawn tasks at work. Use it in a way that can help you maintain a constant state of flow in your work. Else distraction will ensue. Same for any other autonomous work sub task like querying/compiling/etc. Figure out a way to maintain a flow state instead of waiting for these kinds of tasks to finish. *

TV → for me what worked was not having any subscriptions. *

I do not believe in time-based blocking. Whether your system is strict or lenient, keep things blocked 24x7. Make it easier if you find yourself failing a lot. Make it harder if you find yourself succeeding a lot. *

If you find yourself craving screens realize that it's the system working. Use that energy/agitation to calm yourself by sitting and doing nothing for a bit and then transmute that energy into propelling yourself into doing something more productive.

But what if you just can not work on the productive areas of your life at the moment? Just sit and do nothing or read books. Do anything tranquil that is not consumptive. As I often say, eradicating consumptive screen time is perhaps better than even mindfulness meditation. As long as you follow it consistently.

In the end, also have a daily habit tracker and put a tick on it whenever you are able to go through the day without breaking your anti consumptive screen time system. Remember, feedback is paramount, not just ticking. If you find yourself not being able to tick several days in a row, make the system easier until you can. You can always tune it back up a bit more once you are consistently beating the "easier" levels.
