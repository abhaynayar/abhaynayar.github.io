2022 - Year in Review

November 18, 2022

This year was an amazing year.

While I did do some things that looked good on the surface, I feel that those are not that interesting to talk about. So this year, I'll instead talk about something that happened only recently — in the month of November, when a switch flipped inside my mind. It was when I truly embodied the idea of memento mori.

Memento mori is Latin for remember that you have to die.

Like most people, I had come across the idea of death as a motivator many times before. But I used to think that it would only make me anxious, and that I should focus on life instead of death. But while reading some Mark Manson recently, I came across this idea again.

So I made a "life in weeks" chart for myself — which shows you how many weeks you've lived, and how many weeks you have left, if you live to be 80 years old. In this chart, each row represents one year, made up of 52 cells representing the weeks.

My life in weeks chart.

Because we humans constantly forget how precious life is, I have bookmarked this page on my phone's homescreen and I look at it quite often. It makes me prioritize things much better, and prevents me from going back to living my life on autopilot.

Of course you don't have to be living on the edge every single moment. That is the kind of thinking that drives most people away from memento mori. Instead, you have to focus on one or two things at a time to improve on, and then realize that most worthwhile change happens over a long enough time horizon.

I'm not exaggerating when I say that this chart actually makes me realize how precious life is, every single time that I look at it. Sometimes I imagine how it would feel like to not exist, and then when I realize, that I am here in this moment, I feel grateful. And that gratitude makes me not want to waste the precious time that I have been given.

Up until a few years ago, I was lying to myself. I was acting indifferent to my true desires. And it was only over the last year that I slowly started getting much more honest with myself about what I truly want.

When you know who you are and what you want, your insecurities stop bothering you, and you start feeling this lightness in your life because then you can drop all pretenses and external expectations. You can only do this if you are brutally honest with yourself. At that point, external standards of comparison don't matter to you anymore. Only your internal compass does. It's not like you stop caring about everything. It's that you care about a very limited set of things, and you don't have time to waste being too afraid to pursue them.

I learnt, that to be truly honest to myself, I have to listen more closely to my body and my mind. So over the last year or so, after constantly asking myself, "is this what makes me happy?", I have come to accept two broad areas that I am going to focus on, from now on — health and relationships.

Everyone knows how important health and relationships are for happiness, so it might look trivial when I list them out like this. But if I'm being honest with myself, I used to have an unhealthy lifestyle and minimal social interaction.

So now, I have been eating really healthy and light food, getting enough fresh air, and enough sunlight, and practicing yoga diligently, among other things. I am basically trying out various things and seeing what suits my body. If something doesn't, I stop doing it.

For nurturing more relationships, I have been increasing my social interaction surface area whenever I have the opportunity. Since I have acknowledged that I want it, I have stopped making excuses on how the world has to change for me, and started taking initiative instead.

Part of the realization that comes with memento mori, is also to not be too harsh on yourself. Acknowledging your deficiencies, and still being grateful for the present moment. Letting yourself be happy, even if you are not there yet. And being content with the realization that you are on your path now.

This is the greatest paradox of life. And this is where true balance lies. You have to improve yourself and pursue what you want — but at the same time you have to loosen your grip on life, because life is too short to be blocking your own shots.

Until next time.
