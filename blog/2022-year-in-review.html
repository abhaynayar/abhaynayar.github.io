2022 - Year in Review

November 18, 2022

This year, I had a realization that spurred a very fundamental shift in my attitude towards what to pursue in life.

If you would have asked me before about what I really wanted in life, I would have said something on the lines of — getting really good at some niche technical field. This is why I spent most of my educational years getting really good at computers — because I genuinely liked it.

Of course I am still very grateful for being able to work in a field that I'm interested in, most people never get to do that. But over the last year or two, I have been wondering about what truly makes me tick.

Memento mori is Latin for remember death.

Although I had come across the concept of memento mori a few times before in my life, I had always felt that thinking about my death would only make me anxious, so I used to ignore it and have the typical reaction of "I'm not afraid of death."

Eventually, I realized that people who are not afraid of death, aren't grateful of how precious life is. That's when I realized that having the fear of death is actually a good thing. Death has a lot to teach us about how to live and spend our time.

So I made a life in weeks chart for myself. In this chart, you can visualize how much of your life you have already lived. Here, each row represents one year, so in total there are 80 rows for the average lifespan. Within a row, we have 52 cells for the weeks. As every week passes, one cell gets filled up automatically. Indicating your slow march towards death.

Of course you don't have to be living on the edge every single moment. That is the kind of thinking that drives most people away from memento mori. Instead, you have to focus on one or two things at a time to improve on, and then realize that most worthwhile change happens over a long enough time horizon.

In a way, memento mori only sets your priorities straight, you still have to work towards them. It forces you to be honest with yourself about what you truly want. And it makes you realize that you don't have enough time to waste — on making excuses, or being too afraid — to pursue what you really care about.

I listened more closely to my body and my mind over the last year or so. After constantly asking myself, "is this what makes me happy?", there appeared to be two things that contributed most towards my happiness — health and relationships.

So that's what I have been focusing on since. Focusing on things that contribute towards my physical health, and focusing on increasing my social interaction surface area. Of course, these things are not easy and they take time. But looking at my life chart filling up every week, prevents me from getting complacent.

Until next time.
